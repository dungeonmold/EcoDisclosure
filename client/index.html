Understanding the Data

Each dataset in an EcoDisclosure report comes from a federal or intergovernmental program. Learn what each program tracks, what findings mean, and where to investigate further.

Contamination & Hazardous Sites
What it tracks

The National Priorities List (NPL) is the EPA's list of the most serious uncontrolled or abandoned hazardous waste sites in the United States. Sites are proposed, investigated, and listed under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), commonly known as Superfund. Listing triggers a formal cleanup process funded by responsible parties or federal funds.

Typical contaminants

Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, mercury, chromium), volatile organic compounds (benzene, trichloroethylene), polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), petroleum hydrocarbons, pesticides, and radioactive materials depending on the site's industrial history.

What proximity means

Being within 1–2 miles of a listed Superfund site does not automatically mean a property is contaminated. Risk depends on the specific contaminants, groundwater flow direction, soil permeability, and cleanup status. Sites currently under active remediation pose different risks than sites with completed cleanups.

Health implications

Long-term exposure to Superfund contaminants is associated with elevated cancer risk, neurological effects (especially in children from lead exposure), kidney and liver damage, and reproductive harm. Risk pathways include groundwater intrusion into drinking water, soil vapor intrusion, and direct contact with contaminated soil.

What it tracks

Brownfields are real property where the presence or potential presence of a hazardous substance, pollutant, or contaminant complicates expansion, redevelopment, or reuse. The EPA's Assessment, Cleanup, and Redevelopment Exchange System (ACRES) tracks sites receiving federal brownfield grants and assessments. These are generally less severe than Superfund sites — many are former gas stations, dry cleaners, industrial parcels, or commercial properties.

Typical contaminants

Petroleum products (from underground storage tanks), dry cleaning solvents (PCE/TCE), lead paint, asbestos, and light industrial chemicals. Specific contamination depends heavily on prior land use.

What proximity means

Many brownfields are in various stages of assessment and cleanup. A nearby brownfield may have already been cleaned up and redeveloped, or it may still be under investigation. Check the site's status in the ACRES database for current cleanup phase.

Property value context

Proximity to known brownfields is associated with depressed property values, though successfully remediated brownfields can become desirable development sites. Many urban neighborhoods contain former brownfields now converted to parks, housing, or commercial use.

EPA Brownfields Program ↗ ACRES Database ↗
What it tracks

The Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) regulates the management of hazardous waste from cradle to grave. The EPA's RCRAInfo database tracks facilities that generate, treat, store, or dispose of hazardous waste. Active RCRA facilities include manufacturing plants, chemical companies, metal fabricators, and other industrial operations currently handling regulated waste.

Types of facilities

Large quantity generators (hospitals, manufacturers), treatment storage and disposal facilities (TSDFs), transporters, and small quantity generators. TSDFs and large generators pose the highest potential risk from accidental releases.

What proximity means

Unlike Superfund, RCRA facilities are operating businesses handling waste legally under permit. Risk depends on the facility type, compliance history, and whether there have been any reported releases. Facilities with enforcement actions or permit violations warrant closer investigation.

How to investigate

Use EPA's ECHO database to look up a specific facility's compliance history, inspection records, and any violations. RCRA facilities are required to report on waste quantities and disposal methods annually.

What it tracks

The National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit program controls water pollution by regulating point source discharges into U.S. waters. Facilities shown in reports hold active permits to discharge treated wastewater or stormwater. Common permit holders include municipal wastewater treatment plants, industrial manufacturers, construction sites, and agricultural operations.

Common dischargers

Municipal sewage treatment plants (which discharge treated effluent legally), industrial facilities (paper mills, chemical plants, food processors), power plants (thermal discharge), and stormwater systems. Having a permit does not mean a facility is polluting — it means discharge is regulated and monitored.

What to look for

The number of permit holders near a property and their compliance history matters more than mere presence. A single municipal wastewater plant with a clean compliance record is very different from multiple industrial dischargers with violation histories. Use EPA ECHO to check individual facility compliance.

Downstream risk

Properties near permitted discharge points on rivers or streams may be downstream of regulated but potentially impactful discharges. Combined sewer overflows (CSOs) — where stormwater and sewage mix during heavy rain — are a particular concern in older urban areas.

Flood & Climate Risk
What it tracks

The National Flood Hazard Layer (NFHL) is FEMA's official dataset of flood insurance rate maps (FIRMs) for the United States. Flood zones are areas mapped according to their flood risk, determined through hydrologic and hydraulic analyses. These designations directly govern whether property owners are required to purchase flood insurance and what development activities are permitted.

Zone designations explained

Zone AE: High risk — 1% annual chance of flooding (100-year flood). Flood insurance required for federally backed mortgages. Zone A: High risk but no detailed analysis. Zone AO/AH: High risk with shallow flooding. Zone VE: Coastal high risk with wave action — highest hazard. Zone X (shaded): Moderate risk — 0.2% annual chance (500-year flood). Floodway: The channel and adjacent land that must remain open to carry floodwaters — the highest-restriction zone.

Insurance implications

Properties in high-risk zones (A or V designations) with federally backed mortgages are required to carry flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). Annual premiums in AE zones typically range from $700–$3,000+ depending on elevation, structure type, and coverage amount. Properties in Zone X are not required to carry flood insurance but can still flood.

Limitations

FEMA maps are often outdated — many were last updated 10–20 years ago and do not account for climate-driven changes in precipitation or sea level rise. Properties just outside high-risk zones can still flood. Third-party tools like First Street Foundation's Flood Factor provide more current risk assessments.

What it tracks

The US Drought Monitor is a weekly map produced jointly by NOAA, the USDA, and the National Drought Mitigation Center (NDMC) at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln. It synthesizes dozens of drought indicators — precipitation, streamflow, soil moisture, temperature, and expert input — into five intensity categories (D0–D4) covering the contiguous United States.

Intensity categories

D0 — Abnormally Dry: Short-term dryness, possible fire risk. D1 — Moderate Drought: Some crop damage, water shortages begin. D2 — Severe Drought: Crop and pasture losses, water restrictions likely. D3 — Extreme Drought: Major crop losses, widespread water shortages, emergency declarations. D4 — Exceptional Drought: Exceptional and widespread crop/pasture losses, water emergencies.

Property relevance

Prolonged drought affects well water levels, increases wildfire risk, damages landscaping and foundations (shrink-swell soils), and strains municipal water supplies leading to restrictions. In agricultural areas, drought affects surrounding land use and property values. D2+ conditions are associated with significantly elevated wildfire ignition risk.

US Drought Monitor ↗ Drought.gov ↗
What it tracks

The ArcGIS Living Atlas Climate Resilience Planning layer aggregates dozens of census tract–level indicators covering population vulnerability, climate exposure, and resilience capacity. Data comes from the Census Bureau's American Community Survey, EPA air quality monitoring, NOAA land surface temperature satellite imagery, the USFS tree canopy dataset, and First Street Foundation flood models.

Key indicators

Population Vulnerability Index: Composite of poverty rate, minority population percentage, elderly population, disability status, and other social vulnerability factors — normalized to a national percentile. Wildfire Risk to Homes: Average risk score for residential structures in the tract from the USFS Wildfire Risk to Communities dataset. Projected Flood Risk: Percentage of properties in the tract with flood risk today vs. in 30 years under current climate projections (First Street Foundation). Summer Heat (LST): Mean land surface temperature in summer from NOAA satellite data — urban heat island indicator.

Intervention indices

The layer includes priority indices for climate interventions — tree planting, home hardening, cooling centers, flood awareness — ranked at national, state, and county percentiles. High percentile scores indicate tracts where these interventions are most urgently needed based on vulnerability and exposure.

Air Quality & Environment
What it tracks

PM2.5 refers to fine inhalable particles with diameters of 2.5 micrometers or smaller — about 30 times smaller than a human hair. These particles come from combustion (vehicle exhaust, power plants, wood burning, wildfires), industrial processes, and secondary formation from atmospheric reactions. Readings shown in reports come from EPA's AirNow network of regulatory monitors and low-cost sensor networks.

Health effects

PM2.5 is one of the most well-studied air pollutants and is strongly linked to cardiovascular disease, respiratory disease (asthma, COPD), lung cancer, and premature death. Particles are small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. Long-term exposure is associated with reduced life expectancy. Children, elderly, and people with existing heart or lung conditions are most vulnerable.

AQI categories (PM2.5 µg/m³)

0–12 µg/m³ — Good: Air quality is satisfactory. 12.1–35.4 — Moderate: Some pollutants may concern very sensitive individuals. 35.5–55.4 — Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups: Elderly, children, those with heart/lung conditions. 55.5–150.4 — Unhealthy: Everyone may experience health effects. 150.5+ — Very Unhealthy / Hazardous: Health warnings and emergency conditions.

Context for reports

Monitor readings reflect current or recent air quality at the nearest station, which may be several miles away. Local conditions — proximity to highways, industrial facilities, wood stoves — can make actual exposure significantly higher than the nearest monitor suggests. Annual mean PM2.5 exposure (from the Climate Resilience layer) is a better indicator of long-term exposure than current readings.

What it tracks

The National Wetlands Inventory (NWI), produced by the US Fish & Wildlife Service, is the most comprehensive wetlands mapping effort in the United States. It classifies wetlands and deepwater habitats by system (Marine, Estuarine, Riverine, Lacustrine, Palustrine), subsystem, class, and water regime. EcoDisclosure queries the most recent NWI dataset hosted by ESRI's ArcGIS Living Atlas.

Ecological functions

Wetlands provide critical ecosystem services: flood mitigation (absorbing and slowly releasing floodwaters), water quality improvement (filtering nutrients and pollutants), carbon sequestration, groundwater recharge, and habitat for fish and wildlife. Coastal wetlands including salt marshes and mangroves protect shorelines from storm surge.

Regulatory context

Wetlands within about 100 feet of a property can trigger Section 404 of the Clean Water Act, which requires permits for dredging, filling, or development activities that would affect jurisdictional wetlands. Properties intersecting or adjacent to wetlands may have significant development restrictions. Always consult a wetland delineator before purchasing land with wetland features for development.

Property implications

Wetlands near a property can be a positive amenity (scenic views, wildlife, lower density development nearby) or a constraint (development restrictions, flooding risk from seasonal water table fluctuation). Palustrine wetlands (freshwater marshes, swamps, bogs) are the most common type found in residential areas.

Community & Public Health
What it tracks

The County Health Rankings & Roadmaps program, a collaboration between the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation and the University of Wisconsin Population Health Institute, ranks counties within each state on over 50 health outcomes and factors. Data comes from the CDC WONDER database, Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, and other federal sources. Rankings are updated annually and reflect multi-year averages for statistical stability.

Key metrics explained

Life expectancy: Average years of life at birth — the single most comprehensive health outcome indicator. Uninsured rate: Percentage of adults under 65 without health insurance. Drug overdose rate: Age-adjusted deaths per 100,000 from drug overdoses — a proxy for substance use disorder and healthcare access gaps. Food insecurity: Percentage of the population with limited access to adequate nutrition. Park access: Percentage of residents living within a half mile of a park — a measure of active living opportunities.

Environmental justice connection

County health metrics are strongly correlated with environmental exposures. Counties with higher rates of industrial pollution tend to have higher rates of asthma, cardiovascular disease, and premature death. The relationship between environmental burden and health outcomes is the foundation of the environmental justice movement — communities of color and low-income communities bear disproportionate environmental health burdens.

What it tracks

The National Register of Historic Places (NRHP) is the United States' official list of districts, sites, buildings, structures, and objects deemed worthy of preservation for their historical, architectural, archaeological, and cultural significance. Maintained by the National Park Service, the NRHP currently includes over 100,000 properties across all 50 states. EcoDisclosure queries both point locations (individual structures) and polygon boundaries (historic districts) from the NPS mapping service.

Designation types

Individual properties: Specific buildings, structures, or sites listed for their own merit. Historic districts: Defined geographic areas where a concentration of historically significant properties creates a cohesive district — often entire neighborhoods. National Historic Landmarks (NHL): A subset of NRHP properties of exceptional national significance, designated by the Secretary of the Interior.

Property implications

NRHP listing does not restrict what a private property owner can do with their property — it is not a preservation easement. However, listing within a historic district or adjacent to a landmark may create local regulatory constraints through local historic preservation ordinances (HPOs). Federal undertakings affecting listed properties trigger Section 106 review. Listing can also qualify properties for federal historic tax credits (20% of qualified rehabilitation expenses) — significant for renovation projects.

Context for homebuyers

Living near or within a historic district typically correlates with higher property values, architectural character, and neighborhood stability. However, local ordinances in certified local government (CLG) communities may require design review for exterior changes, which can complicate renovations. Check with the local historic preservation commission before purchasing in a designated district.

Terrain & Natural Features
What it tracks

EcoDisclosure queries the USGS National Map contour service (layer 5), which provides contour lines derived from the 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) — the most comprehensive and accurate elevation dataset ever produced for the United States. 3DEP uses airborne lidar technology to capture elevation with resolutions as fine as 1 meter in many areas.

Why elevation matters

A property's elevation relative to surrounding terrain is one of the most important factors in flood risk assessment. Properties at higher elevations relative to nearby waterways, drainage channels, and floodplains have inherently lower flood exposure. Elevation also affects drainage — properties in topographic low points (swales, depressions) collect runoff from surrounding areas. Base Flood Elevation (BFE) — the elevation at which there is a 1% annual chance of flooding — is used to determine flood insurance rates.

Contour intervals

Contour lines connect points of equal elevation. The contour interval (shown in the report) is the vertical distance between adjacent contour lines — tighter lines mean steeper terrain, widely spaced lines mean gentle slopes. In flat areas like coastal plains and river deltas, a 2-foot elevation difference can mean the difference between being in and out of a Special Flood Hazard Area.
